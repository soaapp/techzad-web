---
title: "The Hidden Cost of \"We'll Divest This Later\""
author: Ali
---

A struggling venture rarely announces itself with a single bad quarter. It announces itself with a decision that keeps getting postponed. "We'll divest this later" sounds prudent, even patient. In practice, it often means the hole gets deeper while everyone waits for a better moment to climb out.

I have watched capable teams make this call, and I want to be fair about why. Nobody defers a hard decision out of laziness. They defer it because the story of a turnaround is more comfortable than the arithmetic of a loss.

## Why the delay feels rational

The instinct to wait is not irrational. It is just incomplete.

- **Sunk cost dressed as commitment.** Money and years already spent feel like reasons to continue, when they are actually reasons that no longer inform the decision at all.
- **The recovery narrative.** There is always a plausible next milestone, a pending contract, a new hire who will change everything. Hope is cheap to manufacture and expensive to hold.
- **Optics of the exit.** Divesting looks like admitting failure. Continuing looks like conviction. Leaders are frequently rewarded for the second appearance long after the first would have served the business better.

This does not imply poor judgment. But it does highlight a misalignment between how a decision feels and what it costs.

## Where the real cost hides

The obvious cost is the cash still flowing into a venture that cannot return it. The hidden costs are larger and quieter.

- **Attention.** Your strongest people spend their best hours defending a position instead of building a new one. That opportunity cost never appears on a balance sheet.
- **Compounding, in the wrong direction.** A small loss addressed early is a rounding error. The same loss carried for two more years accumulates obligations, headcount, and expectations that make the eventual exit far harder.
- **Credibility.** Partners and staff notice when a struggling line of business is treated as untouchable. They begin to question every other allocation of trust.

Ironically, the longer you wait to preserve optionality, the less optionality you actually have. A venture is easiest to divest when it still has momentum, and momentum is the first thing to fade.

## A more honest way to decide

The point is not to exit at the first sign of trouble. It is to decide with today's numbers rather than yesterday's investment.

1. **Reset the baseline.** Ask what you would pay to enter this venture today, knowing what you now know. If the answer is nothing, the past spending should not change it.
2. **Name the milestone that would change your mind.** Write it down, with a date. A turnaround with no falsifiable checkpoint is not a plan, it is a wish.
3. **Price the delay.** Estimate what another six months of waiting adds to the eventual cost of exit. Make that number visible in the room.
4. **Separate the people from the project.** A venture may not survive, but the talent within it usually should. Plan their next chapter before, not after, you act.

Is the plan to hold serving a real path to value, or is it mostly serving the comfort of not deciding?

## The closing thought

The hardest ventures to leave are the ones we have narrated to ourselves for too long. The story becomes the asset, and the story does not pay salaries.

Deciding early is not a loss of nerve. It is respect for the capital, the calendar, and above all the people who could be building something that grows. "We'll divest this later" is rarely a strategy. More often it is a bill we postpone, and postponed bills tend to arrive with interest.
